The
Patola
of Patan
______
Crafted exclusively in the town of Patan in Gujarat,
the Patola is a unique kind of Sari. The exquisiteness
of this craft lies in the special "double Ikat" technique
used to create a myriad of intricate patterns. Meticulous
precision, geometric ingenuity and copious amounts of
sheer focus and dedication is what makes every Patola
Sari a prized possession. An heirloom that is passed from
mother to daughter. A Sari that takes almost a year to be
created, a heritage that lasts generations.

Scroll down to experience the uniqueness
of the technique yourself.
Warp and Weft
તાણો અને વાણો
______
The vertical threads in a Sari are known as "Warp"
The horizontal threads are called "Weft". Based on
how many times the pattern repeats vertically and
horizontally the threads are bundled together to
mark the gridlines to create the Pattern.

Let's try to understand this intricate process by
scaling up one motif to see what is happening.

The Dyed Weft
______
The patterns on a Patola Sari are not "printed".
Instead, they emerge out of individual threads
that have been pre-dyed in different parts based
on one of the traditional designs. To form the
as accurately as possible, extreme care needs to
taken in arranging the threads so that they line
up precisely. Both during dyeing and weaving.

Let's look at the journey of the yarn from
being white to being dyed with the pattern.


Marking the Grid
______
The white silk yarn is tied to set up the weft.
Then, the gridlines needed to create the pattern
are marked with a thread soaked with black coal.
Every square unit of the pattern is actually made
of 24 threads! Accordingly, just the warp of the
Sari is made up of 5,760 individual threads.


Tying the pattern
______
Based on the markings, two people begin tying
the areas that form some part of the pattern.
The parts that are tied will not be exposed to
the dye. In this way, only the areas that have
the first color will get dyed. When the threads
are soaked in the dye, the lightest colours are
dyed first and the dark colors are dyed in
the end.

This is to optimise the amount of tying needed
and also as to protect the light colours as they
can't be dyed over darker colours.

In this animation, the pattern has been enlarged
for better visibility. The actual motif is much
smaller and the tying, way more intricate.


Dyeing the
first colour
______
After the first colour has been tied, the thread
bundles are dyed in the first colour. All the
exposed, untied areas absorb the colour of the
dye. This dyeing technique of resisting certain
parts of the yarn from being coloured by tying
them is known as "Ikat"

The Patan Patola stands out from other kinds of
Ikat because of two reasons:

1) They use the "double Ikat" technqiue in which
both the warp AND the weft are dyed.
2) The colour dyes used in Patan are authentic
natural dyes as opposed to the widespread
chemical dyes used elsewhere.
Retaining the
first colour
______
Once the threads are dyed in the first colour,
the second round of tying begins. This time
the tied areas will retain the dyed colour
and the exposed areas will be completely
dyed in the second, darker colour.

This process of tying and dyeing is repeated
according to the number of colours required
in the design.


Dyeing the
second colour
______
After the second round of tying is done,
the thread bundles are dyed again in the
second colour. Being darker, the second
colour covers the previous colour completely
except in the newly tied areas where the
first colour gets retained.
This process of tying and dyeing is repeated
as many times as the number of colours in the
design. In the double Ikat technique used
by Patan Patola weavers, the process is done
for both the warp and the weft.
Removing all
the threads
______
After all the colours have been dyed, it is time
to remove all the threads to see the pattern that
has finally emerged. In this way one of the most
tedious parts of the process is completed..



Arranging, Leasing
and Weaving
______
Once both the warp and weft have been dyed
in all the colours, a team of 2-3 people stretch
and arrange the warp across a traditional loom.
The threads bundles need to be separated and
arranged with extreme care as one misplaced
thread can ruin the whole sequence. The weft
threads are wound onto a reel and attached to
a bamboo shuttle.

The weaving process requires two people, one
person controls the shedding and arranges any
misaligned threads with three needles in a hand.




The other person helps in passing the shuttle
and checking the thread arrangement.

Because of the complexity of the patterns and
the alignment required, only 20-25 centimeters
of a sari can be woven each day. In this way,
the entire process of making a patola, from
preparing the yarns to weaving the Sari can
take about 4-11 months depending on the
pattern. Single Ikat Saris can be produced
faster than the "Double Ikat ones.
